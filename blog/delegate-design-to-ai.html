How to Delegate Design to AI

July 2026

When I needed a personal site, I didn't design it. I asked Claude to generate four completely different layouts and picked the one that looked best. Then I iterated from there.

The Technique

Instead of describing exactly what you want—which requires you to already know what you want—ask for breadth. “Give me four different arrangements of a project card grid.” “Show me three navigation layouts.” The AI is fast enough that generating multiple options costs almost nothing compared to the time you'd spend trying to articulate a single perfect spec.

The key insight is that specification is hard. Most people can't describe a good layout from scratch. But almost everyone can look at four options and point to the one that feels right. You're replacing the hardest part of the process—translating a vague aesthetic sense into precise instructions—with something humans are naturally good at: comparison and selection.

Why This Works

Design is a domain where you know it when you see it, but can't always describe it upfront. Generating options turns the problem from “describe what you want” into “react to what you see.” The latter is much easier and produces better results because you're making decisions with concrete artifacts in front of you, not abstract specifications.

There's a reason architects build physical models and designers create mockups before committing to a direction. The artifact externalizes the decision. You stop trying to simulate the outcome in your head and start responding to something real. AI just makes generating those artifacts nearly free.

How This Site Was Built

This site started as four different HTML page layouts. I picked the structure I liked, then iterated section by section—hero, project cards, blog layout—each time asking for multiple options and selecting. The result is something I'm happy with that I never could have specified from scratch.

The workflow looked like this: generate options, pick the best one, use it as the starting point for the next round. Each round narrowed the design space. By the third or fourth iteration, I had a site that felt coherent and intentional, even though no single version was designed from a unified vision. It emerged from a series of comparative judgments.

When It Doesn't Work

This approach works for visual and structural decisions where taste matters. It's less useful for logic, algorithms, or anything with clear correctness criteria. You don't need four versions of a sorting function. You need four versions of a landing page.

The dividing line is whether the decision is about preference or correctness. If there's a right answer, just ask for the right answer. If the answer depends on what looks good, feels right, or fits a vague aesthetic—generate options and choose. The technique is powerful precisely because it targets decisions that are hard to make analytically but easy to make visually.

Connection to the Series

This is another facet of the same theme running through these posts. In the taste post, I argued that human judgment is the irreplaceable piece—AI can generate, but someone has to decide what's good. Design delegation is that principle turned into a workflow: AI generates the space, human navigates it.
